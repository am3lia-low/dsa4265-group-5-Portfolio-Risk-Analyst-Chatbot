As observed from the confusion matrix in Figure~\ref{fig:lstm_confusion_matrix}, the model performs better in predicting future portfolio downward movements. From Table~\ref{tab:lstm_classification_report}, the model is observed to have very high recall for downward movements, showing that it is able to identify most future downward portfolio directions. However, it has low recall for future upward movements and many future upward trends are misclassified as downward. The model struggles to learn a robust directional signal and the input feature of 60 days log-return does not contain sufficient predictive information for future direction. 

The discrepancy between volatility and direction prediction performance highlights the challenges in financial time series modeling. Volatility is generally easier to predict due to its relatively stable statistical properties like clustering, which allows the model to effectively learn temporal dependencies. Comparatively, future portfolio direction is inherently noisy and less predictable, especially when using relatively short input windows such as 60 days of log returns.

The model is also observed to be more conservative when predicting upward movements as seen from the significantly lower recall for upwards prediction. There are fewer false positives as seen from the higher precision for upwards prediction and the model is more sensitive in predicting downward movements with fewer missed negatives from the higher recall, which is relatively more desirable than the converse in a risk management context.

Therefore, while the LSTM is effective for volatility forecasting, it is less suitable for directional prediction as it struggles to extract meaningful signals for direction due to the high level of noise and weak predictability in short-term portfolio direction. 

\subsection{RAG Retrieval Evaluation}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{rag_eval.png}
    \caption{Recall and MRR at $k \in \{5, 8, 10\}$ across three intent routes.}
    \label{fig:rag_recall_mrr}
\end{figure}

To evaluate retrieval quality, nine queries were designed spanning all four intent routes -  \texttt{full\_analysis}, \texttt{concept\_explanation}, \texttt{trend\_prediction} and \texttt{no\_intent}, at three difficulty levels: Easy, Medium and Hard. Retrieval performance was assessed using Recall@$k$ and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) at $k \in \{5, 8, 10\}$. While MRR measures the rank position of the first relevant result, Recall@$k$ measures how many relevant chunks are surfaced within the top k; the two metrics are therefore complementary, a system can rank its first retrieved chunk perfectly (MRR = 1.0) while still missing out other relevant material (Recall $<$ 1.0).

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:rag_recall_mrr}, MRR equals 1.0 across every intent at every value of k, indicating that whenever a relevant chunk is retrieved, it is consistently ranked first. This confirms that the hybrid retrieval strategy produces well-ordered results.

For \texttt{full\_analysis} queries, Recall@$k$ rises from 0.385 at k=5 to 0.769 at k = 10, reflecting consistent retrieval from a well-populated knowledge store. \texttt{Trend\_prediction} achieves the strongest recall trajectory, reaching near-perfect scores at k=10, suggesting that dense semantic search handles indirectly phrased forward-looking queries effectively.

\texttt{Concept\_explanation} records the lowest Recall@$k$ values; however, this is an artefact of knowledge base construction rather than retrieval failure. Only one relevant chunk exists per concept in the concept store, placing a hard ceiling on the system and motivating future work to expand the concept corpus.

Across all intents, increasing k consistently improves Recall, confirming that the relevant chunks exist in the index and that broader retrieval windows surface them reliably. Overall, the results suggest that the pipeline is well-calibrated for ranking quality, with the primary path to improvement lying in expanding the concept knowledge store rather than in changes to the retrieval mechanism itself.



\subsection{LLM Evaluation}
Finally, we evaluated the workflow functions to verify consistent LLM performance, as these models are prone to variable outputs.

\subsubsection{\texttt{intent\_classifier}}
\paragraph{Evaluation Methodology}

The classifier was evaluated with a hand-crafted test suite of 57 test cases, divided into nine progressively harder sections:


\begin{itemize}
    \item Section 1 — Clear-cut intents: \newline
    Unambiguous examples of all six intents. These establish the performance ceiling for straightforward inputs (e.g. ``Analyse my portfolio'' , ``What is beta?'' , ``Hi'' ).
    
    \item Section 2 — Disambiguation: \newline
    Lexically similar phrasings that should map to different intents. A critical pair tested is:\newline
    ``What is MY Sharpe ratio?'' → \texttt{specific\_metric}  (possessive + metric)\newline
    ``What IS the Sharpe ratio?'' → \texttt{concept\_explanation}  (definitional)

    \item Section 3 — Multi-intent messages: \newline
    Messages that contain two distinct requests simultaneously, requiring the classifier to set both a primary and a secondary intent correctly.\newline
    e.g. ``Analyse my portfolio and explain what VaR means'' \newline
    → \texttt{primary=full\_analysis, secondary=concept\_explanation}

    \item Section 4 — Disguised intents: \newline
    Messages where a greeting or filler phrase wraps an actionable request.\newline
    e.g. ``Hey! Analyse my portfolio please''  → \texttt{full\_analysis}, not \texttt{general\_chat}

    \item Section 5 — Ambiguous/stress tests: \newline
    Vague, minimal, or single-word messages (e.g. ``Risk''  ``Is this good'', ``How's my risk?'' ). These test whether the classifier can infer intent from context and prior conversation history.

    \item Section 6 — Entity extraction: \newline
    Tests validating metric and concept name extraction, not just intent classification.\newline
    e.g. ``How's my downside looking?'' \newline
    → \texttt{specific\_metric} AND extract\texttt{[var\_95, cvar\_95, sortino\_ratio]}

    \item Section 7 — Hard multi-intent edge cases: \newline
    Cases where two apparent intents resolve to the same intent e.g. ``Sell Tesla and analyse what's left'' → \texttt{full\_analysis} only, or where a secondary intent involves a cross-intent pairing.

    \item Section 8 — Adversarial/tricky phrasing: \newline
    Includes: negation (``Don't analyse my portfolio, just tell me what beta means''), informal language with typos (``whats my sharp ratio lol''), extremely long messages with the intent buried at the end, and hypothetical framings that resemble portfolio questions but are conceptual in nature.

    \item Section 9 — Boundary: \texttt{full\_analysis} vs \texttt{specific\_metric}: \newline
    Systematically tests the hardest boundary in the taxonomy. Questions like ``How risky is my portfolio?'' (\texttt{full\_analysis}) vs ``Show me my volatility'' (\texttt{specific\_metric}) differ subtly in scope. This section targets exactly those distinctions.

\end{itemize}

Each test case checked:
\begin{enumerate}[i]
    \item correct primary intent,
    \item correct secondary intent where expected, and
    \item correct entity extraction where specified.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, the classifier achieved approximately 85-88\% accuracy (around 49-50 of the 57 test cases passed). By intent category, \texttt{full\_analysis} achieved the highest absolute count with 21 correctly classified instances, making it the largest intent class, followed by \texttt{specific\_metric} with 16 and \texttt{concept\_explanation} with 10. The remaining categories recorded smaller counts. The confidence scatter plot showed a useful separation: correctly classified tests typically had model confidence of 0.8 to 1.0

It should also be noted that most failures clustered at 0, suggesting that misclassification might also be due to API failures (likely \texttt{503} error). Because when such errors strike, \texttt{intent\_classifier} is designed to default the query to \texttt{general\_chat}. The three test cases misclassified as \texttt{general\_chat} in the rightmost plot, and their near-consecutive occurrences, support this inference.

\subsubsection{\texttt{generate\_explanation}}

\paragraph{Evaluation Methodology}
The explainer was evaluated using an LLM-as-judge framework across a single, balanced portfolio configuration (AAPL, MSFT, SPY, TLT, GLD, VNQ), covering all six intent types. The evaluation had two layers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Layer 1 — Full pipeline execution: \newline
    For each intent type, a realistic test query was sent through the complete pipeline: \texttt{classify\_intent} → \texttt{route\_and\_execute} → \texttt{generate\_explanation} \newline
    This tests the end-to-end behaviour, ensuring the explainer works compatibly in the full pipeline. The \texttt{follow\_up} test case was chained off the actual \texttt{full\_analysis} response to simulate a real conversational sequence.

    \item Layer 2 — LLM-as-judge scoring: \newline
    Two independent models from different providers, Claude Sonnet (claude-sonnet-4-6) and Groq Llama (llama-3.3-70b-versatile), were chosen to guard against self-grading bias. \newline

    They scored the responses on four dimensions, on a 0-5 scale:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Factual accuracy}: Do the numbers and labels in the response match the provided data exactly?
        \item \textbf{Intent alignment}: Does the response directly and fully answer the user’s query?
        \item \textbf{Completeness}: Does the response cover all required aspects for this intent type?
        \item \textbf{Clarity}: Is the response well-structured, written in plain, comprehensible language?

    \end{enumerate}

\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Results}

Results are visualised as dual heatmaps: rows represent intent types, columns represent the four evaluation dimensions, with one heatmap for each judge.

Initial evaluation runs (Figure~\ref{fig:eval_heatmap_before}) revealed two factual accuracy failures, where \newline
 \texttt{trend\_prediction} and \texttt{full\_analysis} scored very poorly on factual accuracy. Possible reasons were hallucinated metrics (since the two intents cited plenty of metrics), and the systematic confusion between two distinct LSTM output fields. The model incorrectly described the "up" direction, which predicts the direction of returns, as "rising volatility".

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{eval_heatmap_before.png}
    \caption{Initial judge scorings for the various responses.}
    \label{fig:eval_heatmap_before}
\end{figure}

To address these failures, targeted measures were added to the system prompt:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A more explicit field definition clarifying that \texttt{predicted\_direction} refers to \texttt{PORTFOLIO RETURNS} direction, not volatility
    \item An inline \texttt{AUTHORISED NUMBERS} annotation block was added to the \texttt{generate\_explanation} prompt, forcing the model to re-read what each forecast field means before generating
    \item Changing \texttt{temperature} for Gemini 2.5 Flash from \texttt{0.7} to \texttt{0.0}
\end{itemize}

After these changes, scores improved substantially in the final run.
